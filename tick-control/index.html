Common Tick Species in Colorado

Several tick species can be found in Colorado, each with its own preferred habitats and potential for disease transmission. While the deer tick (also known as the blacklegged tick), notorious for transmitting Lyme disease, is present in Colorado, other species like the Rocky Mountain wood tick and the American dog tick are more commonly encountered in the Broomfield area. The Rocky Mountain wood tick can transmit Rocky Mountain spotted fever, while the American dog tick is a vector for diseases like tularemia and ehrlichiosis. Understanding which species are present in our region is crucial for developing targeted control strategies. Our technicians are trained to identify these different species and understand their behaviors and life cycles, which informs our treatment approach. Knowing the specific tick species present helps us tailor our treatments for maximum effectiveness against the particular threats they pose. We'll educate you on the types of ticks you might encounter in Colorado and the potential health risks associated with each, empowering you with knowledge to better protect yourself and your family during your outdoor activities.

Understanding Tick Habitats and Behavior

Ticks don't just appear out of nowhere; they have specific habitats where they thrive and behaviors that increase the likelihood of encountering them. Understanding these aspects is key to effective prevention and control. Ticks prefer areas with tall grass, brush, leaf litter, and wooded edges. They are often found in transition zones between lawns and natural areas, as well as along trails and paths. Ticks don't fly or jump; they typically wait on vegetation for a host to brush against them – a behavior known as 'quests.' Deer, rodents, and birds are common hosts that can bring ticks onto your property. Our tick control strategies target these preferred habitats, disrupting the tick's ability to quest and find hosts on your property. By modifying your landscape to make it less attractive to ticks and their hosts, you can further reduce your risk. Simple measures like keeping your lawn mowed, clearing leaf litter, and creating a buffer zone between wooded areas and your lawn can make a big difference.
